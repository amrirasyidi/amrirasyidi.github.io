Reflection on Freelancing on Upwork

Published on [March 08th, 2026]

A while ago, I started picking up some freelance project through upwork, now I would like to write some reflection of those.

I know upwork since a long time ago, back when upwork has some sort of selection process, hence not everybody will be accepted to start a side gigs there. I know it because I was one of that person. Back in 2020 I saw a linkedin profile that list upwork in their professional experience, out of curiosity I check the website and decided to sign up. At that time, I only have roughly 2 years of experience as a professional. After filling some forms to sign up, I waited for some sort of approval/confirmation, and upon receiving it I was noticed that I'm not accepted. Imagine applying for a side job and got declined lol.

Fast forward to a while ago, I found again some linkedin profiles that mentioned upwork as their professional experience, some even wrote a post about their upwork's projects and achievements, then I got reminded, "hey, I tried applying for upwork before, let me revisit!".

Surprisingly (or not, because I didn't even have a chance to work on upwork before), I can "revive" my account and I got quite a lot of "connect" (this is a currency in upwork), and I can apply to the posted jobs on upwork. I haven't really check the most updated t n c, but I don't think there are restriction on who can sign up and apply to work on a project on upwork (please correct me if I'm wrong, but I'm not really in the mood for fact checking lol).

Got excited because of the chance to work on a side gig, I applied and used my connect carelessly and before I knew it, I already running low on my connects. And this is where the first lessons come.

1. Connect is a limited resource, use it wisely.

If you are applying to an upwork project. Some connects will be required, take it as some capital you need to start a business (or a project really). One way to use is wiser it by carefully select which project you are applying to, one way to filter it is by looking at:

  • Payment verified badge: to me this adds some sort of certainty that the client will indeed pay me in the agreed terms.
  • Have a good review: some clients despite having payment verified badge got some bad reviews, beware especially if the review is about impossible timeline or requirement and also late it payment.
  • Have spent some money on upwork: this indicates that the client is not foreign with how upwork works.
This information is provided in the "Find work" page as some sort of statistics of the prospective client.

2. Think about 0 to 1

If you just starting, the first thing you should aim is to have a completed upwork project. This has 2 benefit at least for me. One, this will serve as my upwork portfolio, showcasing that I was able to finish a project successfully. Second, and this is actually more important, it gives you a sense of achievement, and it can snowball your motivation to keep going!

Here are some things worth considering when looking for your "low-hanging fruit" (i.e. your 0 to 1):

  • Find something you can finish quickly
    You can literally type "quick" + "your keyword" in the search for a job, e.g. "quick python". The search result will then serve you with projects that potentially can be done quickly. Of course, not all the search result can actually be finished quickly, the result are based on the project title and description after all.
    One of my earliest project was finished in less than a day.
  • Don't think too much about the budget
    As this is a low-hanging fruit, budget shouldn't really be your first priority, as long as the expected deliverable meet the timeline and budget, a $5-10 is a way to go. One of my earliest project was only for $10.
  • Few proposal
    Look for projects that have only few proposals, this can potentially increase the probability of the client to read your proposal.
  • Freshly posted
    This is related to the previous point of few proposal. On top of that, there's a possibility that the client is still online on upwork after posting the job and can reply immidiately to your proposal.
To sum it up, the tips above are ways to find projects with a higher chance to start.

3. What to wrote on the cover letter

Except stated otherwise in the project description, there's no standard way on what information you should include in the cover letter. However, I would imagine from the perspective of the client, I would like to know these information:

  • Credibility
    Briefly introduce yourself in regards or relevancy to the project. I usually state where do I work and my role, sometimes I elaborate what exactly I'm doing in my role that is relevant to the project.
  • Relevant skills, project and experience
    After establishing your credibility, elaborate a bit more and show some proofs, this could be a link to git repository, your personal showcasing-website, etc. Sometimes I again elaborate on certain portfolio that is the most relevant to the project I'm appliying to.
  • Call-to-action
    I always create a remarks like "Would you be available for a quick chat to discuss the details?". This prompts the client to just chat me. Sometimes I even ask clariying question upfront, showing that I know what I'm doing and again prompts the client to answer those questions.
💡

Keep it brief. All of my "Hired" or "Seen by client" proposals are less than 170 words. This is good both for you and the client as you can save both of your time.

In some cases client will attach some sample data that you can look into. Sometimes, I can literally finish the project before even submitting my proposal. In those cases, I also attached the finished work. Basically saying, "here's what you want, now give me the money" lol.

4. Maximize your profile page

I would like to imagine, after the client read my proposal, they will go to my upwork profile page and look around. This is another opportunity to showcase my capability. So make sure you completed the necessary information to send a "sense of safety" to your client, saying "I'm a professional, I would finish the job, and your project is safe with me". Here's somethings you can do:

  • Make sure your "Title" is representative of what you are doing
  • Make a short version of "Profile overview" (the one below the "Title")
    People will always scroll from top to bottom of the profile page, and there will be part of the overview that is hidden if it exceed certain length of characters (indicated by the "more" button).
    What I would like to do is to prioritize the most important information in that "always-showing" part of the overview, then add more details afterwards.
  • The rest is typical complete profile: fill in your portofolio part (include image if any!), testimonial, certification, and so on.

5. Communicate well with your client

Remember that this is a two-sided project, you are not the only one that have needs here, your client also need their project to be finished, you are on equal footing as your client. Here's some things you can consider when communicating with your client:

  • Know your limit
    I completely realize that upwork for me is a side-gig, I have a full-time job and I don't want to compromise it by working on the side-gig during my full-time job working hour. And I make sure I communicate this to my client, I can only work x hours a week and that is most likely night time or during the weekend.
  • Manage expectation
    This is related to the previous point. However there's more to "time" in the "expectation". Make sure you agreed on the expected deliverable as detailed as possible before even accepting the offer. This is to ensure that you know what to deliver and the client understand what product to expect.
    On some of my project, I put into details the expected deliverable (then discuss with the client if they are agree with it) and break them down into milestones.
  • Use milestones
    On top of a way to communicate deliverables, milestones are also a way to safe guard your self from scammy client. Using milestone you can detect if the client is really willing to pay for the project. Don't move to the next milestone if the client seems unreasonably reluctant to the agreed amount on that milestone.
    Exception of course if for some reason, you don't deliver the agreed deliverable for that milestone and the client question it.
  • Soft sale/Upsale
    After finishing the project, you can give a closing remarks to your client, expressing how do you enjoy working on the project. Then you can also tell them what other expertise you may have.
    I got ne of the project I worked on because I do this exactly, the client then interested to involve me on another project they had.
  • Build connection
    Similar to the point above, it is not impossible for the client to get back to you, after all they have a good experience working with you. So make sure to also build rapport/connection with the clients, even the one that you don't get to start the project with.
  • Know your project's worth
    I have some experience of client downplaying the worth of the project. In the end, I didn't pick the project as it seems to me they don't really understand the project (and the risk) themself. So if a client try to lowball you, you can reply (respectfully) with objective counter-points.

6. More on connect

There are some information regarding connect, that I know by experiencing it myself, I believe this information is provided somewhere on the upwork website, but I don't really read those things lol.

  • There is a chance to win 10 free connects per month.
    However, you need to spent some connect by bidding on a project to be eligible for these 10 free connects.
  • Some successfull proposal might reward you certain amount of connect.
    For some project, if your proposal got accepted and you got the offer, you will rewarded by some connects, I don't know what kind of project have this kind of feature.

Closing

All in all, I enjoyed my experience on side-questing on upwork, I learn a lot about being self-reliant, exposed to variety of projects, and add more values to my portfolio. But I realized, I still exchange my time with money, so I think this is not exactly a good strategy if you want to have an additional source of income. My time is limited and I know I want to spend it on something else besides working.
